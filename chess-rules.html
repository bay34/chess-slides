A Beginner's Guide to the Game of Kings

♟️

Learn the fundamentals of chess and start your journey to becoming a strategic player

The Chessboard & Pieces

The Board

Chess is played on an 8×8 board with 64 squares alternating between light and dark colors. The board should be positioned so each player has a light square in the bottom-right corner.

The Pieces

King (♔) - 1 per player
The most important piece
Queen (♕) - 1 per player
The most powerful piece
Rook (♖) - 2 per player
Moves in straight lines
Bishop (♗) - 2 per player
Moves diagonally
Knight (♘) - 2 per player
Moves in an L-shape
Pawn (♙) - 8 per player
The foot soldiers

How the Pieces Move

  • King: Moves one square in any direction (horizontal, vertical, or diagonal)
  • Queen: Moves any number of squares in any direction (horizontal, vertical, or diagonal)
  • Rook: Moves any number of squares horizontally or vertically
  • Bishop: Moves any number of squares diagonally
  • Knight: Moves in an L-shape: two squares in one direction and one square perpendicular. Knights can jump over other pieces
  • Pawn: Moves forward one square (or two squares on its first move). Captures diagonally forward one square
Special Rule: No piece (except the knight) can jump over other pieces. When you capture an opponent's piece, you remove it from the board and take its square.

Special Moves & Rules

Castling

A special move involving the king and a rook. The king moves two squares toward the rook, and the rook jumps over to the square next to the king. This can only be done if neither piece has moved, there are no pieces between them, and the king is not in check.

En Passant

A special pawn capture. If an opponent's pawn moves two squares forward and lands beside your pawn, you can capture it as if it had only moved one square (on your very next turn only).

Pawn Promotion

When a pawn reaches the opposite end of the board, it must be promoted to a queen, rook, bishop, or knight (usually a queen).

Check and Checkmate

Check: When your king is under attack. You must move out of check immediately.

Checkmate: When your king is in check and there's no legal move to escape. This ends the game!

Winning the Game

Objective

The goal of chess is to checkmate your opponent's king. This means putting their king in a position where it is under attack and cannot escape capture.

Other Ways the Game Can End

  • Stalemate: A draw where a player has no legal moves but is not in check
  • Draw by agreement: Both players agree to a draw
  • Insufficient material: Neither player has enough pieces to checkmate
  • Threefold repetition: The same position occurs three times

Beginner Tips

  • Control the center of the board
  • Develop your pieces early (don't just move pawns)
  • Protect your king (consider castling early)
  • Think before you move - consider your opponent's threats
  • Practice regularly to improve your strategy!
